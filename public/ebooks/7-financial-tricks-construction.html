01 The Perfect Business Structure

Estimated savings: $18,000 - $35,000/year

I'd love to tell you exactly what the perfect business structure is, but it's not quite that simple. Even within the construction industry, there are a lot of variables. But I can tell you that there's a perfect business structure for YOUR business, and finding it is key.

Depending on how the business is owned (single owner, partners, etc.), how many employees you have, and your annual revenue, the perfect structure will look different. But setting up the right structure can save you big on your taxes, protect you from liability issues, and help you keep more of your hard-earned profits.

For construction businesses, here's what we typically see:

  • LLC: Great liability protection, especially important when you have employees on job sites
  • S-Corporation Election: Opens up massive tax strategies for established businesses
  • C-Corporation: Sometimes beneficial for larger construction companies planning significant reinvestment

S-Corp status allows you to increase your tax savings on things like your health insurance costs, vehicle expenses (huge for construction!), equipment costs, and much more. It also allows you to adjust your ratio of salary/profit distributions, which can make a portion of your income exempt from payroll tax—potentially saving you thousands!

Pass-through entities like S-Corps, sole proprietors, and partnerships may also be able to deduct 20 percent of their Qualified Business Income (Section 199A deduction). This can be a huge deduction and save you A LOT on your tax bill.

Every business is unique, so we walk each of our clients through possible structures and weigh the pros and cons to find the perfect fit. If you're currently set up with the wrong structure, finding the best one could save you $18,000-$35,000 a year and continue to save you money year after year.

02 Save on Tax-Free Expenses with an Accountable Plan

Estimated savings: $3,000 - $8,000/year

As a construction business owner, you're probably paying for a lot of things out of pocket that could be tax-deductible business expenses:

  • Fuel and vehicle maintenance
  • Tools and equipment
  • Safety gear and work boots
  • Home office expenses
  • Phone and internet used for business
  • Continuing education and licensing fees

You may not even realize just how quickly it adds up. You need to be reimbursed for these out-of-pocket expenses, but there are right and wrong ways to do it.

The solution? Setting up an Accountable Plan.

An Accountable Plan is a structured plan to reimburse employees (including owner/employees) for out-of-pocket business expenses. With a properly set up plan in place, you can deduct the reimbursements as a business expense, and the payments won't be considered taxable income to you.

You don't need to establish a plan in any certain, formal way, but you DO need to have a solid plan in place that meets IRS reimbursement standards, or you risk having those payments reclassified as wages, triggering more personal taxes and more payroll taxes for the business.

This is also a big help if you have employees paying for union dues, steel-toed boots, tool belts, or equipment out of pocket. They'll get tax-free reimbursement, and you'll save yourself payroll tax on those payments as well.

03 Master Job Costing or It Will Ruin You

Estimated savings: $25,000 - $75,000/year

In the construction business, knowing your true cost per job is a survival skill. If your cost tracking is out of control (or worse, you don't track it at all), there's no way you can manage your profits or bid accurately on new projects.

What is job costing? It's tracking ALL costs associated with each specific project:

  • Materials and supplies
  • Direct labor (your crew's time on that specific job)
  • Subcontractor costs
  • Equipment rental or usage
  • Permits and fees

Before working with us, one contractor thought he was making 25% profit margins. When we implemented proper job costing, we discovered he was actually LOSING money on certain types of projects. His estimates weren't accounting for true labor time, material waste, and callbacks.

Another client was under-bidding commercial jobs because they weren't factoring in the full cost of insurance, bonding, and administrative overhead for larger projects. They were getting plenty of work but barely breaking even.

Fortunately, there are amazing tech tools that can integrate your project management system with QuickBooks and help you track the profitability of each job. Examples include:

  • Buildertrend - Comprehensive project management with built-in financials
  • CoConstruct - Perfect for custom builders and remodelers
  • Foundation - Great for commercial contractors
  • QuickBooks Projects - Built right into QuickBooks Online

Want to know which projects are profitable and which are draining your cash? Need to adjust your bidding strategy? Is it time to drop certain types of work? The answer starts with proper job costing. This could easily save you $25,000-$75,000 a year by helping you avoid unprofitable work and bid more accurately.

04 Optimize Your Labor Costs

Estimated savings: $15,000 - $40,000/year

Labor is typically one of your top three expenses in construction, so you need to maximize efficiency and make strategic decisions about your workforce.

Employees vs. Subcontractors: Do you know the difference?

Many construction businesses misclassify workers as independent contractors when they should be employees. Unfortunately, we don't get to pick and choose who's an employee and who's a contractor—there are clear IRS guidelines. If you misclassify, that could be considered payroll tax fraud and can pop up during audits, insurance claims, or even workers' comp investigations.

Red flags for misclassification:

  • You control when and how they work
  • You provide all tools and equipment
  • They work exclusively for you
  • They work on an ongoing basis, not project-by-project

On the flip side, strategically using legitimate subcontractors can help you:

  • Scale up and down based on project pipeline
  • Reduce payroll tax burden and benefits costs
  • Access specialized skills without full-time hires
  • Reduce workers' comp insurance costs

We help construction clients analyze their workforce and create a strategic mix of employees and subcontractors that maximizes profitability while staying fully compliant with IRS and state regulations.

Additionally, accurate payroll tracking by job site helps you identify which crews are most efficient and where you might have productivity issues. Without this data, you're bidding blind on labor costs.

05 Pay Yourself the Right Way

Estimated savings: $8,000 - $20,000/year

Hopefully this will also mean "pay yourself A LOT," but it starts with paying yourself correctly and consistently.

Too many construction business owners pay themselves whatever is "left over" at the end of the month. Some months that's great. Other months (especially during slow seasons or when you're waiting on receivables) that means you don't get paid at all.

The solution: Pay yourself first.

Depending on your business structure, there are different ways to pay yourself as an owner:

  • Owner's Draw: For sole proprietors and partnerships
  • Guaranteed Payments: For multi-member LLCs
  • W-2 Salary + Distributions: For S-Corps (with strategic tax planning opportunities)

An S-Corp, for example, may require you to take a reasonable salary rather than taking all your compensation as profit distributions. This is the IRS's way of preventing tax evasion, and you need to be careful. Within those rules, though, there's a lot of room for strategy. Adjusting your salary and distributions can save you thousands in payroll taxes.

Not only do you need to pay yourself the right way, but you should also be paying yourself FIRST—before you pay suppliers, before you upgrade equipment, before you do anything else. Your bookkeeper can help you plan and budget your finances to be sure you're actually getting paid consistently.

06 Maximize Equipment and Vehicle Deductions

Estimated savings: $10,000 - $50,000/year

As a construction business owner, you probably have significant investments in vehicles, tools, and equipment. The tax code offers HUGE opportunities to deduct these expenses—but only if you know the rules and track everything properly.

Key tax strategies for construction equipment:

  • Section 179 Deduction: Write off up to $1,220,000 (2024 limit) of equipment purchases in the year you buy them
  • Bonus Depreciation: Additional first-year deduction for qualifying property
  • Vehicle Deductions: Choose between standard mileage or actual expenses—whichever saves you more
  • Heavy Vehicle Exception: Trucks and vans over 6,000 lbs GVW get special treatment

Many construction business owners miss these deductions because:

  • They don't keep good mileage logs for business vehicle use
  • They don't know they can deduct equipment in the year of purchase
  • They mix personal and business use without proper allocation
  • They forget to track smaller tool and equipment purchases

Example: If you purchase a $60,000 truck for your business, you could potentially write off the entire amount in year one (instead of depreciating it over 5-7 years), saving you $15,000+ in taxes depending on your tax bracket.

We help our construction clients set up systems to capture all these deductions automatically, ensuring nothing slips through the cracks. This is one of the easiest ways to save tens of thousands in taxes.

07 Don't Run Your Business Without a Budget and Cash Flow Plan

Estimated savings: $20,000 - $60,000/year

For many people, budgeting feels like punishment. Restrictive. It's like a money diet for your business. In reality, budgeting should be a proactive process that takes away stress and actually gives you more freedom.

Budgeting is a non-negotiable part of a successful construction business. Without a budget, it's just a matter of how long you can hold on before cash flow problems sink you. Construction has unique cash flow challenges:

  • Large upfront material costs before you get paid
  • Progress billing that lags behind expenses
  • Seasonal revenue fluctuations
  • Retainage held back for months
  • Long payment terms from commercial clients

One contractor we worked with was on the brink of collapse despite having a full project pipeline. They had plenty of work but kept running out of cash to pay suppliers and crews. Why? Because they weren't forecasting cash flow—they were just hoping money would be there when they needed it.

We helped them build a 13-week rolling cash flow forecast that showed them exactly when money was coming in from receivables and when major expenses were due. This allowed them to:

  • Negotiate better payment terms with suppliers
  • Plan which jobs to start based on available cash
  • Set up a line of credit BEFORE they desperately needed it
  • Identify negative cash flow periods in advance

What a good construction budget includes:

  • Revenue forecast by project and month
  • Direct costs (materials, labor, subs) by job
  • Overhead expenses (insurance, rent, admin staff)
  • Equipment purchases and payments
  • Tax payments (don't forget quarterly estimates!)
  • Owner compensation

Think about it: you wouldn't start a project without a plan and timeline. Why would you run your business finances without one?

BookkeepingPro Solutions creates budgets using the data already in your accounting software. We regularly review your budget and your goals to make sure they line up. That way you can make smart money moves before it's too late. A good budget and cash flow plan can easily save you $20,000-$60,000 a year by helping you avoid costly financing, late payment penalties, and cash crunches that force you to pass on profitable work.
